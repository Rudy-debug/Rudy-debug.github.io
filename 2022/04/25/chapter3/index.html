Eulerian and Lagrangian Description of Conservation Laws

The conservation laws can be measured by either a Lagrangian (material volume, MV, \(\mathbf{x}\left(t,\mathbf{x}_{0}\right)\)) or an Eulerian (control volume, \(\mathbf{v}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)\)) approach.The two specifications are related by

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{v}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\left(t,\mathbf{x}_{0}\right)\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\mathbf{x}\left(t,\mathbf{x}_{0}\right) \label{eq1} \end{equation} \]

Substantial Versus Local Derivative

The derivative of a field variable \(\phi\left(t,\mathbf{x}\left(t\right)\right)\), which may be a scalar or a vector quantity. \(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t}\) represents the Eulerian derivative, and

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{D \phi}{D t}=&\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} \frac{d t}{d t}+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \underbrace{\frac{d x}{d t}}_{u}+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} \underbrace{\frac{d y}{d t}}_{v}+\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} \underbrace{\frac{d z}{d t}}_{w} \\ =&\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t}+u \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}+v \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y}+w \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} \\ =&\underbrace{\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t}}_{\begin{array}{l}local \quad rate \\ of \quad change\end{array}}+\underbrace{\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \phi}_{\begin{array}{l}convective \quad rate \\ of \quad change\end{array}} \end{aligned} \label{eq2} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{D \mathbf{v}}{D t}=\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t}+\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{v} \label{eq3} \end{equation} \]

Note: \(\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{v}=\left(\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla\right) \mathbf{v}\)

Reynolds Transport Theorem

Let \(B\) be any property of the fluid (mass, momentum, energy, etc.) and let \(b={dB}/{dm}\) be the intensive value of \(B\) (amount of \(B\) per unit mass) in any small element of the fluid. The instantaneous total change of \(B\) in the material volume (\(MV\), Lagrangian) is equal to the instantaneous total change of \(B\) within the control volume (\(V\), Eulerian) plus the net flow of \(B\) into and out of the control volume through its control surface (\(S\)). The Reynolds transport theorem gives:

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\frac{d B}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\frac{d}{d t}\left(\int\limits_{V(t)} b \rho d V\right)+\int\limits_{S(t)} b \rho \mathbf{v}_{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} d S \label{eq4} \end{equation} \]

Note: for equation \(\eqref{eq4}\), the control volume is moving and deformable.

where \(\mathbf{v}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)\) the velocity of the fluid, \(\mathbf{v}_{s}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)\) the velocity of the deforming control volume surface, and \(\mathbf{v}_{r}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)\) the relative velocity by which the fluid enters/leaves the control volume [i.e., \(\mathbf{v}_{r}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)=\mathbf{v}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)-\mathbf{v}_{s}\left(t,\mathbf{x}\right)\)]

For a fixed control volume, \(\mathbf{v}_{s}=0\), and using Leibniz rule:

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{d}{d t}\left(\int\limits_{V} b \rho d V\right)=\int\limits_{V} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(b \rho) d V \label{eq5} \end{equation} \]

Then, \(\eqref{eq4}\) simplifies to

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\frac{d B}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\int\limits_{V} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(b \rho) d V+\int\limits_{S} b \rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n} d S \label{eq6} \end{equation} \]

using the divergence theorem (Gauss' theorem)

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\frac{d B}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho b)+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} b)\right] d V \label{eq7} \end{equation} \]

using \(\eqref{eq2}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\frac{d B}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{D}{D t}(\rho b)+\rho b \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right] d V \label{eq8} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Mass (Continuity Equation)

In the absence of mass sources and sinks, for Lagrangian:

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\frac{d m}{d t}\right)_{M V} = 0 \label{eq9} \end{equation} \]

In this case, \(B=m\) and \(b=1\), uing \(\eqref{eq8}\), for Eulerian

\[ \begin{equation} \int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{D \rho}{D t}+\rho \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right] d V =0 \label{eq10} \end{equation} \]

the differential form of the mass conservation or continuity equation as following

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{D \rho}{D t}+\rho \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} =0 \label{eq11} \end{equation} \]

the flux form, uing \(\eqref{eq7}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \int\limits_{V}\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot \left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]\right) d V=0 \label{eq12} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot \left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]=0 \label{eq13} \end{equation} \]

for incompressible flow, \({D\rho}/{Dt}=0\)

\[ \begin{equation} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}=0 \label{eq14} \end{equation} \]

using the divergence theorem (Gauss' theorem)

\[ \begin{equation} \int\limits_{S}\left(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n}\right) d S=0 \label{eq15} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Linear Momentum

Newton's law in Lagrangian coordinates can be written as

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\frac{d\left(m\mathbf{v}\right)}{dt}\right)_{MV}=\left(\int\limits_{V}\mathbf{f}dV\right)_{MV} \label{eq16} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \left(\int\limits_{V}\mathbf{f}dV\right)_{MV} = \int\limits_{V}\mathbf{f}dV \label{eq17} \end{equation} \]

Non-Conservative Form

In this case, \(B=m\mathbf{v}\) and \(b=\mathbf{v}\), uing \(\eqref{eq8}\), for Eulerian

\[ \begin{equation} \int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{D}{D t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\left[\rho \mathbf{v} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]-\mathbf{f}\right] d V=0 \label{eq18} \end{equation} \]

the differential form

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{D}{D t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\left[\rho \mathbf{v} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]=\mathbf{f} \label{eq19} \end{equation} \]

regrouping the \(\eqref{eq19}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{D}{D t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\left[\rho \mathbf{v} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]=\mathbf{f} \Rightarrow \\ \rho\frac{D\mathbf{v}}{D t}+\mathbf{v}\frac{D\rho}{D t}+\left[\rho \mathbf{v} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]=\mathbf{f} \\ \rho\frac{D\mathbf{v}}{D t}+\underbrace{\mathbf{v}\left(\frac{D\rho}{D t}+\rho \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)}_{Continuity}=\mathbf{f} \\ \end{aligned} \label{eq20} \end{equation} \]

In the absence of mass sources and sinks, \(\frac{D \rho}{D t}+\rho \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} =0\) \(\eqref{eq11}\), then

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rho\frac{D\mathbf{v}}{D t}+\underbrace{\mathbf{v}\left(\frac{D\rho}{D t}+\rho \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)}_{Continuity}=\mathbf{f} \Rightarrow \\ \rho\frac{D\mathbf{v}}{D t}=\mathbf{f} \Rightarrow \\ \rho\left[\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t}+\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{v}\right]=\mathbf{f} \end{aligned} \label{eq21} \end{equation} \]

Conservative Form

The conservative (or flux) version, uing \(\eqref{eq7}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\}-\mathbf{f}\right] d V=0 \label{eq22} \end{equation} \]

the differential form

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =\mathbf{f} \label{eq23} \end{equation} \]

Note: \(\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\) is the dyadic product.

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{f}=\mathbf{f}_{s}+\mathbf{f}_{b} \label{eq24} \end{equation} \]

where: \(\mathbf{f}_{s}\) the surface forces, \(\mathbf{f}_{b}\) the body forces

Surface Forces

the forces acting on its surface are due to pressure and viscous stresses

\[ \begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}\Sigma_{x x} & \Sigma_{x y} & \Sigma_{x z} \\ \Sigma_{y x} & \Sigma_{y y} & \Sigma_{y z} \\ \Sigma_{z x} & \Sigma_{z y} & \Sigma_{z z}\end{array}\right) \label{eq25} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}=-\left(\begin{array}{ccc}p & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & p & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & p\end{array}\right)+ \left(\begin{array}{ccc}\overbrace{\Sigma_{x x+p}}^{\tau_{x x}} & \tau_{x y} & \tau_{x z} \\ \tau_{y x} & \overbrace{\Sigma_{y y+p}}^{\tau_{y y}} & \tau_{y z} \\ \tau_{z x} & \tau_{z y} &\overbrace{\Sigma_{z z+p}}^{\tau_{z z}}\end{array}\right)=-p\mathbf{I}+\boldsymbol{\tau} \label{eq26} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} p=-\frac{1}{3}\left(\Sigma_{x x}+\Sigma_{y y}+\Sigma_{z z}\right) \label{eq27} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \underbrace{\int\limits_{V} \mathbf{f}_{s} d V=\int\limits_{S}}_{surface \quad force} \mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot \mathbf{n}\underbrace{ d S=\int\limits_{V} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{\Sigma} d V}_{(Gauss' \quad theorem)} \Rightarrow \mathbf{f}_{s}=[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{\Sigma}]=-\nabla p+[\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}] \label{eq28} \end{equation} \]

Note: \(\mathbf{f}_{s}\) has two forms. \(\mathbf{f}_{s}=[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{\Sigma}]\) for \(\int\limits_{V} \mathbf{f}_{s} d V\) ; and \(\mathbf{f}_{s}= \mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot d\mathbf{S}=\mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS\) for \(\int\limits_{s} \mathbf{f}_{s} d S\)

\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{\Sigma}=\nabla \cdot \left(-p\mathbf{I}+\boldsymbol{\tau}\right)=-\nabla \cdot\left(p\mathbf{I}\right)+\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}=-\nabla p+\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\)

\(\begin{aligned}-\nabla \cdot\left(p\mathbf{I}\right)=&-\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \mathbf{i}+\frac{\partial}{\partial y} \mathbf{j}+\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \mathbf{k}\right) \cdot \left(\mathbf{i i} p+\mathbf{i j} 0+\mathbf{i k} 0+\mathbf{j i} 0+\mathbf{j j} p+\mathbf{j k} 0+\mathbf{k i} 0+\mathbf{k j} 0+\mathbf{k k} p\right) \\ =&- \frac{\partial p}{\partial x} \mathbf{i}+\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} \mathbf{j}+\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} \mathbf{k} \\ =&-\nabla p \end{aligned}\)

Body Forces

Gravitational Forces

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{f}_{b}=\rho \mathbf{g} \label{eq29} \end{equation} \]

System Rotation

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{f}_{b}=\underbrace{-2 \rho[\boldsymbol{\varpi} \times \mathbf{v}]}_{\text {Coriolisforces }}-\underbrace{\rho[\boldsymbol{\varpi} \times[\boldsymbol{\varpi} \times \mathbf{r}]]}_{\text {Centrifugalforces }} \label{eq30} \end{equation} \]

The general conservative form of the momentum equation

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =-\nabla p+[\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}]+\mathbf{f}_{b} \label{eq31} \end{equation} \]

Stress Tensor and the Momentum Equation for Newtonian Fluids

To proceed further with the momentum equation, the type of fluid should be known in order to relate the stress tensor \(\boldsymbol{\tau}\) to the flow variables.

For a Newtonian fluid, the stress tensor is a linear function of the strain rate

\[ \begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\tau}=\mu\{\nabla\mathbf{v}+\left(\nabla\mathbf{v}\right)^{\mathrm {T}}\}+\lambda\left(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right) \mathbf{I} \label{eq32} \end{equation} \]

where \(\mu\) is the molecular viscosity coefficient, and \(\lambda\) represents the bulk viscosity coefficient which usually set equal to \(\lambda=-(2/3)\mu\).

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{I}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right] \label{eq33} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\tau}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} & \mu\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right) & \mu\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right) \\ \mu\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right) & 2 \mu \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}+\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} & \mu\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}\right) \\ \mu\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right) & \mu\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}\right) & 2 \mu \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}+\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\end{array}\right] \label{eq34} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \left[\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\right]=&\nabla \cdot\{\mu\left(\nabla \mathbf{v}+(\nabla \mathbf{v})^{\mathrm{T}}\right)\}+\nabla(\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}) \\ =&\left[\begin{array}{l}\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[2 \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[\mu\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)\right]+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left[\mu\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)\right] \\ \left.\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)\right]+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[2 \mu \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}+\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left[\mu\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}\right)\right] \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\mu\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)\right]+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[\mu\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}\right)\right]+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left[2 \mu \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}+\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]\end{array}\right] \end{aligned} \label{eq35} \end{equation} \]

\(\nabla \cdot \left(\lambda\left(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right) \mathbf{I}\right)=\nabla \left(\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)\), proved by equation \(\eqref{eq28}\)

Then,

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =-\nabla p+\nabla \cdot\{\mu\left[\nabla \mathbf{v}+\left(\nabla \mathbf{v}\right)^{\mathrm{T}}\right]\}+\nabla(\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v})+\mathbf{f}_{b} \end{aligned} \label{eq36} \end{equation} \]

The expanded form

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =\nabla \cdot\{\mu \nabla \mathbf{v}\}-\nabla p+\underbrace{\nabla \cdot\{\mu \left(\nabla \mathbf{v}\right)^{\mathrm{T}}\}+\nabla(\lambda \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v})+\mathbf{f}_{b}}_{\mathbf{Q}^{\mathbf{v}}} \end{aligned} \label{eq37} \end{equation} \]

rewritten

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =\nabla \cdot\{\mu \nabla \mathbf{v}\}-\nabla p+\mathbf{Q}^{\mathbf{v}} \end{aligned} \label{eq38} \end{equation} \]

  • for incompressible flows, using \(\eqref{eq14}\), \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}=0\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =-\nabla p+\nabla \cdot\{\mu\left[\nabla \mathbf{v}+\left(\nabla \mathbf{v}\right)^{\mathrm{T}}\right]\}+\mathbf{f}_{b} \end{aligned} \label{eq39} \end{equation} \]

  • If the viscosity is constant for incompressible flows

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mu \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & {\left[2 \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right]+\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)\right]+\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left[\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)\right] } \\ &=\mu\left[\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} v}{\partial y x}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial z^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} w}{\partial z x}\right] \\ &=\mu\left[\left(\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial z^{2}}\right)+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} v}{\partial y x}+\frac{\partial^{2} w}{\partial z x}\right] \\ &=\mu\left[\left(\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial z^{2}}\right)+\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right)}_{=0 \quad for \quad incompressible }\right] \\ &=\mu\left(\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial z^{2}}\right) \end{aligned} \label{eq40} \end{equation} \]

then,

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =-\nabla p+\mu\nabla^{2} \mathbf{v}+\mathbf{f}_{b} \end{aligned} \label{eq41} \end{equation} \]

Note: assuming that there is no mass sources and sinks for equation \(\eqref{eq39}\) and \(\eqref{eq41}\)

  • for inviscid flows for incompressible and compressible

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} =-\nabla p+\mathbf{f}_{b} \end{aligned} \label{eq42} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Energy

Defining the total energy \(E\) of the material volume at time \(t\) as the sum of its internal and kinetic energies, then \(E\) can be written as

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} E=m\left(\hat{u}+\frac{1}{2} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right) \end{aligned} \label{eq43} \end{equation} \]

where \(\hat{u}\) is the fluid specific internal energy (internal energy per unit mass).

The first law of classical thermodynamics:

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \left(\frac{d E}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\dot{Q}-\dot{W} \end{aligned} \label{eq44} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} B=E \Rightarrow b=\frac{d E}{d m}=\hat{u}+\frac{1}{2} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}=e \end{aligned} \label{eq45} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \left(\frac{d E}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\dot{Q_V}+\dot{Q_S}-\dot{W_b}-\dot{W_S} \end{aligned} \label{eq46} \end{equation} \]

where \(\dot{Q_{S}}\) is the rate of heat transferred across the surface of the element and \(\dot{Q_{V}}\) represents the generated/destroyed heat (e.g., due to a chemical reaction) within the material volume. \(\dot{W_{S}}\) is the rate of work done by the surface forces and \(\dot{W_{b}}\) represents the rate of work done by the body forces.

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dot{W_b}=-\int\limits_{V} \left(\mathbf{f}_{b} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)dV \\ \dot{W_S}=-\int\limits_{S} \left(\mathbf{f}_{S} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)dS \\ \end{aligned} \label{eq47} \end{equation} \]

replacing \(\mathbf{f}_{S}\) by \(\mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS\) using equation \(\eqref{eq28}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dot{W_S}=&-\int\limits_{S} \left(\mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)dS=\underbrace{-\int\limits_{S} \left[\mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right]\cdot \mathbf{n} dS=-\int\limits_{V} \nabla \cdot \left[\mathbf{\Sigma} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]dV}_{Gauss' \quad theorem} \\ =&-\int\limits_{V} \nabla \cdot \left[\left(-p\mathbf{I}+\boldsymbol{\tau} \right) \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]dV \end{aligned} \label{eq48} \end{equation} \]

rewriting

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dot{W_S}=&-\int\limits_{V} \left(-\nabla \cdot \left[p \mathbf{I} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]\right)dV \\ =&-\int\limits_{V} \left(-\nabla \cdot \left[p \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]\right)dV \end{aligned} \label{eq49} \end{equation} \]

Note: \(\mathbf{I} \cdot \mathbf{v}= \mathbf{v}\)

If \(\dot{q_V}\) represents the rate of heat source or sink within the material volume per unit volume and \(\dot{q_s}\) the rate of heat transfer per unit area across the surface area of the material element

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dot{Q_V}=&\int\limits_{V} \dot{q_V} dV \\ \dot{Q_S}=&\underbrace{-\int\limits_{S} \dot{q_s} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS =-\int\limits_{V}\nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} dV}_{Gauss' \quad theorem} \end{aligned} \label{eq50} \end{equation} \]

using equation \(\eqref{eq7}\) and \(\eqref{eq46}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \left(\frac{d E}{d t}\right)_{M V}=&\int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho e)+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} e)\right] d V \\ =&-\int\limits_{V}\nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} dV+\int\limits_{V} \dot{q_V} dV \\ &+\int\limits_{V} \left(-\nabla \cdot \left[p \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]\right)dV+\int\limits_{V} \left(\mathbf{f}_{b} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)dV \end{aligned} \label{eq51} \end{equation} \]

Collecting terms together

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho e)+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} e) + \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} +\nabla \cdot \left[p \mathbf{v}\right]-\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]- \mathbf{f}_{b} \cdot \mathbf{v}-\dot{q_V}\right] d V=0 \end{aligned} \label{eq52} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho e)+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} e) =- \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} -\nabla \cdot \left[p \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]+ \mathbf{f}_{b} \cdot \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq53} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Energy in Terms of Specific Internal Energy

the dot product of the momentum equation \(\eqref{eq23}\) with the velocity vector

\[ \begin{equation} \left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\rho \mathbf{v}\right]+\nabla \cdot\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\}\right] \cdot \mathbf{v} =\mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{v} \label{eq54} \end{equation} \]

\(\begin{aligned}\left[ \nabla \cdot \{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \right] \cdot \mathbf{v} =& \mathbf{v} \cdot \left[ \nabla \cdot \{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \right] = \nabla \cdot \left[\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right]-\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\}:\nabla\mathbf{v}=\nabla \cdot \left[\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right]-\{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \cdot \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} \\ =&\nabla \cdot \{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \cdot \mathbf{v}-\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}=\nabla \cdot \{\rho \mathbf{v} \mathbf{v}\} \cdot \mathbf{v}-\rho \mathbf{v} \left(\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \right) \cdot \mathbf{v}\end{aligned}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)-\rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot \left[\rho \left(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)\mathbf{v}\right] -\rho\mathbf{v} \cdot\left[\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla \right) \mathbf{v}\right] =\mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{v} \label{eq55} \end{equation} \]

\(\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]-\mathbf{v}\cdot\left(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\right)=\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}\) for symmetric tensor

\(\boldsymbol{\tau}:\mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}=\boldsymbol{\tau}\cdot\mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{v}\)

Rearranging

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)+\nabla \cdot \left[\rho \left(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)\mathbf{v}\right]- \mathbf{v} \cdot \rho\underbrace{\left[ \frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} +\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla \right) \mathbf{v}\right]}_{=\mathbf{f} \quad Eq\eqref{eq21} } =\mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{v} \label{eq56} \end{equation} \]

Note: assuming that there is no mass sources and sinks

the flow kinetic energy

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)+\nabla \cdot \left[\rho \left(\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)\mathbf{v}\right]= \underbrace{-\mathbf{v} \cdot\nabla p+\mathbf{v} \cdot\left[\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\right]+\mathbf{f}_{b}\cdot\mathbf{v}}_{ Eq\eqref{eq24} } \label{eq57} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} \right)&+\nabla \cdot \left[\rho \left(\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right)\mathbf{v}\right] \\ &= -\nabla\cdot\left[p\mathbf{v}\right]+p\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}+\underbrace{\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]-\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}}_{for \quad symmetric \quad tensor }+\mathbf{f}_{b}\cdot\mathbf{v} \end{aligned} \label{eq58} \end{equation} \]

Subtracting Eq \(\eqref{eq58}\) from Eq \(\eqref{eq53}\), the energy equation with specific internal energy

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial\rho\hat{u}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} \hat{u}) =- \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} -p\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}+\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq59} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Energy in Terms of Specific Enthalpy

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \hat{u}=\hat{h}-\frac{p}{\rho} \end{aligned} \label{eq60} \end{equation} \]

Substituting Eq \(\eqref{eq60}\) for \(\hat{u}\) in Eq \(\eqref{eq59}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial\rho\hat{h}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} \hat{h}) =- \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} +\frac{Dp}{Dt}+\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq61} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Energy in Terms of Specific Total Enthalpy

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} e=\hat{u}+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}=\hat{h}-\frac{p}{\rho}+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}=\hat{h_0}-\frac{p}{\rho} \end{aligned} \label{eq62} \end{equation} \]

Substituting Eq \(\eqref{eq62}\) for \(e\) in Eq \(\eqref{eq53}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho \hat{h_0})+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} \hat{h_0}) =- \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} +\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot \left[\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v}\right]+ \mathbf{f}_{b} \cdot \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq63} \end{equation} \]

Conservation of Energy in Terms of Temperature

Assuming \(\hat{h}\) to be a function of \(p\) and \(T\), the fluid is expected to be Newtonian.

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} d\hat{h}=\left(\frac{\partial \hat{h}}{\partial T}\right)_{p}dT+\left(\frac{\partial \hat{h}}{\partial p}\right)_{T}dp \end{aligned} \label{eq64} \end{equation} \]

the ordinary equilibrium thermodynamics relation

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \left(\frac{\partial \hat{h}}{\partial p}\right)_{T}=\hat{V}-T\left(\frac{\partial \hat{V}}{\partial T}\right)_{p} \end{aligned} \label{eq65} \end{equation} \]

where the \(\hat{V}\) is the specific volume

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} d\hat{h}=c_p dT+\left[\hat{V}-T\left(\frac{\partial \hat{V}}{\partial T}\right)_{p}\right]dp \end{aligned} \label{eq66} \end{equation} \]

using the left side of Eq \(\eqref{eq61}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial\rho\hat{h}}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \hat{h}\right) =&\rho\frac{\partial\hat{h}}{\partial t}+\hat{h}\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}+\hat{h}\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \right)+\rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \hat{h} \\ =& \rho\left(\frac{\partial\hat{h}}{\partial t}+\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla \hat{h}\right)+\hat{h}\underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \right)\right)}_{=0 \quad Eq \eqref{eq13}} \\ =& \rho\frac{D\hat{h}}{D t}=\rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t}+\rho\left[\hat{V}-T\left(\frac{\partial \hat{V}}{\partial T}\right)_{p}\right]\frac{Dp}{D t} \\ =&\rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t}+\rho\left[\frac{1}{\rho}-T\left(\frac{\partial (1/\rho)}{\partial T}\right)_{p}\right]\frac{Dp}{D t} \\ =&\rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t}+\rho\left[\frac{1}{\rho}-T\left(\frac{-\frac{1}{\rho^2}\partial \rho}{\partial T}\right)_{p}\right]\frac{Dp}{D t} \\ =&\rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t}+\left[1+\left(\frac{\frac{1}{\rho}\partial \rho}{\frac{1}{T}\partial T}\right)_{p}\right]\frac{Dp}{D t} \\ =&\rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t}+\left[1+\left(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}\right)_{p}\right]\frac{Dp}{D t} \end{aligned} \label{eq67} \end{equation} \]

Substituting Eq \(\eqref{eq67}\) into Eq \(\eqref{eq61}\) gives the energy equation with T

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t} =- \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} -\left(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}\right)_{p}\frac{Dp}{D t}+\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq68} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rho c_p\frac{DT}{D t} &= \rho c_p\underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}+\mathbf{v}\cdot \nabla T\right)}_{=\frac{DT}{D t}} + T c_p \underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \right)\right)}_{=0 \quad Eq \eqref{eq13}} \\ &=c_p\left(\rho\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}+\rho\mathbf{v}\cdot \nabla T+T\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}+T\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \right)\right) \\ &=c_p\left(\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right)\right) \\ &=- \nabla \cdot\dot{q_s} -\left(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}\right)_{p}\frac{Dp}{D t}+\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq69} \end{equation} \]

The heat flux \(\dot{q_s}\) is the heat transfer by diffusion (molecular level), which is governed by Fourier's law

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dot{q_s}=-\left[k\nabla T\right] \end{aligned} \label{eq70} \end{equation} \]

where \(k\) is the thermal conductivity of the substance, and the medium is isotropic. However some solids are anisotropic for which Eq \(\eqref{eq70}\) is replaced by

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dot{q_s}=-\left[\mathbf{k} \cdot \nabla T\right] \end{aligned} \label{eq71} \end{equation} \]

where \(\mathbf{k}\) is a second order symmetric tensor called the thermal conductivity tensor.

using Eq \(\eqref{eq70}\), then Eq \(\eqref{eq69}\) becomes

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} c_p\left(\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right)\right)=\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right]-\left(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}\right)_{p}\frac{Dp}{D t}+\boldsymbol{\tau}:\nabla \mathbf{v}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq72} \end{equation} \]

using \(\eqref{eq34}\), the expression for \(\left(\tau: \nabla \mathbf{v}\right)\) can be written as

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \left(\tau: \nabla \mathbf{v}\right)=&\lambda\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right)^{2} \\ +&\mu\left(\begin{array}{c}2\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)^{2}+2\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right)^{2}+2\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right)^{2} \\ +\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\right)^{2} \end{array}\right) \end{aligned} \label{eq73} \end{equation} \]

Let:

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \Psi=\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right)^{2} \end{aligned} \label{eq74} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \Phi=&2\left[\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right)^{2}\right] \\ &+\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\right)^{2} \end{aligned} \label{eq75} \end{equation} \]

Then,

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} c_p\left(\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right)\right)=\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right]-\left(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}\right)_{p}\frac{Dp}{D t}+\lambda\Psi+\mu\Phi+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq76} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} c_p\left(\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right)\right)=&c_p\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+c_p\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right) \\ =&\frac{\partial \rho c_p T}{\partial t}-\rho T\frac{\partial c_p}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho c_p \mathbf{v} T \right)-\rho \mathbf{v} T\cdot \nabla c_p \\ =&\frac{\partial \rho c_p T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho c_p \mathbf{v} T \right)-\rho T\underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial c_p}{\partial t}+\mathbf{v}\cdot \nabla c_p\right)}_{\frac{D c_p}{D t}} \\ \frac{\partial \rho c_p T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho c_p \mathbf{v} T \right)=&\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right] \\ &+\underbrace{\rho T\frac{D c_p}{D t}-\left(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}\right)_{p}\frac{Dp}{D t}+\lambda\Psi+\mu\Phi+\dot{q_V}}_{Q^{T}} \end{aligned} \label{eq77} \end{equation} \]

Then,

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial \rho c_p T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho c_p \mathbf{v} T \right)=\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right] +Q^{T} \end{aligned} \label{eq78} \end{equation} \]

The dissipation term \(\Phi\) has negligible values except for large velocity gradients at supersonic speeds. Moreover, for incompressible fluids the continuity equation implies that \(\Psi = 0\) and because the density is constant it follows that \(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}=0\).

  • incompressible fluid flow

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial \rho c_p T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho c_p \mathbf{v} T \right)=&\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right] +\underbrace{\rho T\frac{D c_p}{D t}+\dot{q_V}}_{Q^{T}} \end{aligned} \label{eq79} \end{equation} \]

  • for the case of a solid

the density is constant, the velocity is zero, and if changes in temperature are not large then the thermal conductivity may be considered constant

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rho c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=k \nabla^{2} T +\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq80} \end{equation} \]

  • for ideal gases

\(pv=nRT \Rightarrow T=\frac{p}{nR\rho} \Rightarrow dT=-\frac{p}{nR\rho^2}d\rho\)

\(\frac{\partial (\ln\rho)}{\partial (\ln T)}=\frac{T}{\rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial T}=\frac{\frac{p}{nR\rho}}{\rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{-\frac{p}{nR\rho^2}d\rho}=-1\)

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} c_p\left(\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right)\right)=\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right]+\frac{Dp}{D t}+\lambda\Psi+\mu\Phi+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq81} \end{equation} \]

If viscosity is neglected (i.e. the flow is inviscid), Eq \(\eqref{eq81}\) can be further simplified to

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} c_p\left(\frac{\partial \rho T}{\partial t}+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} T \right)\right)=\nabla \cdot\left[k\nabla T\right]+\frac{Dp}{D t}+\dot{q_V} \end{aligned} \label{eq82} \end{equation} \]

General Conservation Equation

\(\rm{Term \enspace I= Term \enspace II + Term \enspace III}\)

\(\rm{Term \enspace I}\)：change of \(\phi\) over time \(\Delta t\) within the material volume (\(MV\))

\(\rm{Term \enspace II}\)：surface flux of \(\phi\) over time \(\Delta t\) across the control volume (\(V\))

\(\rm{Term \enspace III}\)：source/sink of \(\phi\) over time \(\Delta t\) within the control volume (\(V\))

Note: \(\phi\) represents any intensive property, which has the same meaning of \(b\) in Equation \(\eqref{eq4}\). And using Equation \(\eqref{eq7}\) for the fixed control volume, the \(\rm{Term \enspace I}\) can be written as

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rm{Term \enspace I} =& \left(\frac{d B}{d t}\right)_{M V}=\frac{d }{d t}\left(\int\limits_{MV}\left(\rho\phi\right)dV\right) \\ =&\int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho \phi)+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} \phi)\right] d V \end{aligned} \label{eq83} \end{equation} \]

Note: \(\phi=b\) and \(\rho\) is the fluid density and \(V\) the volume of the control volume of surface area \(S\). The term \(\rho\mathbf{v}\phi\) represents the transport of \(\phi\) by the flow field and is denoted by the convective flux, i.e.,

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{J}_{convection}^{\phi}=\rho\mathbf{v}\phi \end{aligned} \label{eq84} \end{equation} \]

Note: the convective flux is related to the velocity.

For \(\rm{Term \enspace II}\), the mechanism causing the influx/out flux of \(\phi\) is due to diffusion, which is produced by molecular collision

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{J}_{diffusion}^{\phi}=-\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi \end{aligned} \label{eq85} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rm{Term \enspace II}=\underbrace{-\int\limits_{S}\mathbf{J}_{diffusion}^{\phi} \cdot dS=-\int\limits_{V}\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_{diffusion}^{\phi} dV}_{Gauss' \enspace theorem} = \int\limits_{V}\nabla \cdot \left(\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi\right) dV \end{aligned} \label{eq86} \end{equation} \]

source term

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rm{Term \enspace III}=\int\limits_{V}Q^{\phi} dV \end{aligned} \label{eq87} \end{equation} \]

Thus the conservation equation can be expressed as

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho \phi)+\nabla \cdot(\rho \mathbf{v} \phi)\right] d V=\int\limits_{V}\nabla \cdot \left(\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi\right) dV+\int\limits_{V}Q^{\phi} dV \end{aligned} \label{eq88} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \int\limits_{V}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \phi\right)+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \phi\right)-\nabla \cdot \left(\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi\right)-Q^{\phi}\right] d V=0 \end{aligned} \label{eq89} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \phi\right)+\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \phi\right)-\nabla \cdot \left(\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi\right)-Q^{\phi}=0 \end{aligned} \label{eq90} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \phi\right)+\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}^{\phi}-Q^{\phi}=0 \end{aligned} \label{eq91} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{J}^{\phi}=\mathbf{J}^{\phi,C}+\mathbf{J}^{\phi,D}=\rho \mathbf{v} \phi-\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi \end{aligned} \label{eq92} \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \underbrace{\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\rho \phi\right)}_{unsteady \enspace term}+\underbrace{\nabla \cdot\left(\rho \mathbf{v} \phi\right)}_{convection \enspace term}=\underbrace{\nabla \cdot \left(\Gamma^{\phi}\nabla\phi\right)}_{diffusion \enspace term}+\underbrace{Q^{\phi}}_{source \enspace term} \end{aligned} \label{eq93} \end{equation} \]
